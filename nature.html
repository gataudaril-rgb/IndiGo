Nature

A quick look at India’s natural beauty—mountains, deserts, forests, coasts, and rivers.

Why it feels vast

From snow to sand, India stretches across multiple landscapes. Each region has its own colors, temperature, and rhythm—so traveling even a short route can feel like crossing different worlds.

Himalaya

Mountains

India's north is crowned by the Himalayas, formed by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates. Many peaks rise above 7,000 m, and snowfields feed glaciers that give birth to major rivers. Weather changes quickly with altitude, so valleys can feel mild while ridgelines stay icy. Kangchenjunga (8,586 m) is often cited as the highest peak in India.

Thar

Desert

India's main desert is the Thar Desert (mostly Rajasthan and parts of Gujarat), covering about 200,000 km². The landscape is defined by rolling dunes, sparse shrubs, and dry winds that can raise dust in seconds. Days can be very hot while nights cool down fast, especially outside the monsoon months. Desert towns and fort cities are shaped by water scarcity and seasonal travel.

Ghats

Hills & forests

Hilly nature is shaped by the Western Ghats and Eastern Ghats. The Western Ghats stretch about 1,600 km and cover roughly 140,000 km², catching monsoon clouds and creating cooler, wetter slopes. These forests are known for high biodiversity, waterfalls, and layered canopies, with hill stations and tea/spice-growing regions along the ridges. Misty mornings and sudden rain are common, especially during monsoon season.

Coast

Coasts & rivers

India has long coastlines along the Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal (often cited at around 7,500 km). Beaches, cliffs, and backwaters meet busy ports and fishing villages. Major rivers like the Ganga (~2,525 km) create fertile plains and deltas, while mangroves and estuaries protect shorelines and support wildlife. Coastal life is strongly seasonal—monsoon winds, tides, and river floods all shape daily routines.
